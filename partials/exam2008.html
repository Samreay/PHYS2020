  1. Thermal equilibrium is transitive. Temperature is a value which is the same for all objects in thermal equilibrium.

  2. The first law states essentially that energy is conserved. It allows us to define the thermodynamic state function of (internal) energy.

  3. For mechanical explanations: work is moving volume against a certain pressure. It is just a reformulation of force times by distance. Mechanically, I don't know how to describe heat. Disordered kinetic energy?

    For molecular explanations: work is energy that is being transferred due to synchronous motion of particles. Heat is the disordered vibration/movement of molecules.

    The difference between internal energy and enthalpy is that enthalpy takes into account any mechanical work against an external pressure.

  4. The second law says that the entropy of the universe (or isolated systems) never decreases. But if we are talking constant $T$ and $P$ we are probably talking about the thermodynamic identity of the Gibbs free energy. For something a bit more rigorous, consider the following, where we have made use of the fact the Gibbs free energy decreases in a spontaneous reaction...

    \begin{eqnarray*} G &=& H - TS \\ dG + T dS &=& dH \\ \text{As } dG &\le& 0 \\ T dS &\ge& dH \end{eqnarray*}
