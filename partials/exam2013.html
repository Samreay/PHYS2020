Question 1: Consider a system consisting of one ole of monatomic ideal gas undergoing the following cycle:
  1. isochoric heating
  2. adiabatic expansion
  3. isochoric cooling
  4. adiabatic compression back to its original state
  1. Sketch a PV diagram for this process. Indicate for each step whether the heat and work done is positive, negative or zero.
  2. Along which segments is new entropy created? What type of engine is this likely to be?

a) So, here is the diagram. Draw mental arrows clockwise if it helps around the shape.

As to the stages...

  1. As no volume change, no work is being done. But as PV is increasing, temperature must be increasing ($PV = nRT$), so Q must be flowing into the system.
  2. As volume changes, and increases, negative work is done ($W = -PV$). As it is adiabatic, $Q=0$.
  3. No volume, no work. Decreasing in $PV$, so negative $Q$.
  4. Work is done, volume decreasing, so positive work is done. $Q=0$, just like in ii).

b) So entropy is created when we add heat to the system, or expand it to a greater volume. Thus entropy is created in steps i) and ii). This is an Otto engine.

Question 2:
  1. Derive an expression for the internal pressure $\pi_T$ of a van der Waals gas in terms of its $a$ and $b$ coefficients.
  2. Explain the pysical significance of this result. In your answer, contrast the van der Waals behaviour with that of an ideal gas

  1. Right, so just jumping onto the formula sheet now, where it gives us the van der Walls pressure and an equation with $\pi_T$.

    \begin{eqnarray*} \pi_T &=& T \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V - P \\ &=& \frac{nRT}{V-nb} - \frac{nRT}{V-nb} + a\left(\frac{n}{V}\right)^2 \\ &=& a \frac{n^2}{V^2} \end{eqnarray*}

  2. Physical significance is that a real gas always has internal pressure. The significance is that, unlike an ideal gas in which temperature can be used to find the total internal energy, a real gas will have energy stored internally in the gas pressure as well.

For sufficiently thick glaciers (greater than 50m), the large stresses on the ice cause it to deform and flow as a plastic. Glaciers in warmer areas can undergo basal sliding, whereby a layer of meltwater underneath the glacier allows it to slide with little friction over the bedrock.
  1. Applying a large pressure to ice will cause the melting point of ice to decrease. What unusual feature of the phase diagram of water makes this possible?
  2. Calculate the melting point of the ice at the bottom of a 50m thick glacier, to determine whether the weight of the ice above the meltwater has a significant effect on the temperature at which it can form.

  1. The unusual feautre is that the phase boundary between ice and water is a steep negative gradient, not a positive gradient. This means that the more pressure you apply to ice, the lower the melting point becomes.

  2. Okay, so first thing to note is that we are going to use the Clausius-Clapeyron relation, which requires $\Delta P$, $T$, $L$ and $\Delta v$, which is the change in specific volume. Now, the change in specific volume is in cubic metres per kilogram, so this is just the inverse change in density (kg/m$^3$) we are given in the question. Also note that the pressure is easily found if we consider a rectangular prism 1m on the side and 50m tall of ice: $P = F/A = V\rho g/A = g \rho h a^2 / a^2 = g\rho h$.

    \begin{eqnarray*} \frac{dP}{dT} &=& \frac{L}{T\Delta v} \\ \frac{dP}{dT} &=& \frac{L\Delta \rho}{T} \\ \Delta T &=& \frac{\Delta P T}{L\Delta \rho} \\ \Delta T &=& \frac{g \rho h T}{L\Delta \rho} \\ \Delta T &=& \frac{(9.81\;\mathrm{m/s}^2)\cdot(916.7\;\mathrm{kg/m}^3)\cdot (50\;\mathrm{m}) \cdot (273\;\mathrm{K})}{(334\;000 \;\mathrm{J/kg})\cdot ((916.7-1000)\;\mathrm{m}^3\mathrm{/kg})} \\ \Delta T &=& -4.4 \;\mathrm{K} \end{eqnarray*}

Question 6: Thermodynamics of a rubber band
Suppose you suspend a mass from the end of a rubber band and heat the rubber. An interesting feature is that, under these conditions (constant tension), it will contract as it is heated, such that $$\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial T}\right)_f < 0,$$, where $f$ is the tension in the rubber and $L$ is its length.
  1. When you stretch a rubber band by applying a tension force, what kind of work are you doing on it? Write down the first law for this system as an expression for $dU$, ignoring any changes in volume.
  2. Derive the thermodynamic identity for the free energy $F = U - TS$ and hence prove the maxwell relation $$\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial L}\right)_T = - \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial T}\right)_L.$$
  3. Using this Maxwell relation and the fact that rubber contracts as it is heated under constant tension, show that the entropy of the rubber will decrease as it is stretched slowly (isothermally). Note that Young's modulus is positive - increasing the tension at constant temperature will increase the length.
  4. Give an explanation of this result from a microscopic point of view. In your answer explain why this is different to an ideal gas, whose entropy will increase with expansion.

  1. You are doing mechanical work stretching the rubber band. If we look at energy from entropy and temperature, and tension and stretch, we get $dU = f dL + T dS$.

  2. So we can just write out the derivative term and sub out $dU$.

    \begin{eqnarray*} F &=& U - TS \\ dF &=& dU - TdS - SdT \\ &=& f dL + T dS - T dS - S dT \\ &=& f dL - S dT \end{eqnarray*}

    Now, as $F$ is a state function, we can apply the symmetry of second differentials. We can also see that $f = \frac{\partial F}{\partial L}_T$ and $S = - \frac{\partial F}{\partial T}_L$.

    \begin{eqnarray*} \frac{\partial}{\partial T} \left( \frac{ \partial F}{\partial L} \right)_T &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial L} \left( \frac{ \partial F}{\partial T} \right)_L\\ \frac{\partial}{\partial T} f &=& \frac{\partial}{\partial L} (-S)\\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial T} &=& -\frac{\partial S}{\partial L}\\ \end{eqnarray*}

  3. Right, so we just use that maxwell relation and put $\partial L$ on the other side to get $\Delta S = - \Delta L \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial L}\right)_L$. In this scenario, if we are stretching the rubber, $\Delta L > 0$, and (due to Young's modulus being positive), $\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial L}\right)_L > 0$. Which means that $\Delta S < 0$, so entropy is decreasing.

  4. There is only one way to fully straighten a kinked molecule. But there are innumerable ways to scrunch it back up. Thus the scrunched up state has far higher entropy. A gas on the other hand, always has more possible microstates with more space to play with.
